Now that you’ve had a chance to explore the data on your own, let’s revisit participant NM0004S4.

As we can recall, participant NM0004S4 had an average displacement of 3.772 mm/s during silence and 7.458 mm/s during EDM.

However, if we look at participant NM0004S9, an individual in the same group as NM0004S4, we can see that they had an average displacement of 28.476 mm/s during silence and 11.371 mm/s during EDM.

When we look at the average displacement by genre for all participants in our data, we can see that it is slightly higher when participants are in silence, meaning that participants strayed further away from their initial starting point during periods of silence.

All of the other genres have a similar average displacement over time, indicating that on average, no singular genre resulted in significantly more head movement than any of the other genres.

So, why would participants move more during silence? This could be for one of two reasons:

1: The data we are using is only a subset of the data from the entire experiment. It is possible that our sample might consist of participants who tend to move more during silence.

2: In the absence of auditory stimulation, people often shift, tap their feet, adjust their posture, or otherwise drift more. Silence often feels uncomfortable, whereas music gives people a rhythm to lock onto, so people might tend to make more micro-movements during periods of silence.

Furthermore, while different music genres evoke different styles of movement, their overall displacement still ends up about the same. This might be because individual differences within each genre overtake any small genre‐level effects.
